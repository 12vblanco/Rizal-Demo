The Filipino physician, polymath, and hero José Rizal lived in Germany from February 1886 to May 1887, primarily to pursue his specialization in ophthalmology. While there, he completed his seminal novel Noli Me Tángere, published in Berlin in March 1887. He also immersed himself in the intellectual life of the city, engaging with the Berlin Society of Anthropology, Ethnology, and Prehistory and the Berlin Geographical Society, attending lectures, meeting leading scientists, including Rudolf Virchow, and presenting and publishing a paper on Tagalog poetry.

Rizal simultaneously worked on translations of Friedrich Schiller's Wilhelm Tell and the Malay volume of Theodor Waitz's Anthropologie der Naturvölker, while maintaining lively correspondence with his Filipino colleagues and Ferdinand Blumentritt. These exchanges often explored the challenges of interpreting concepts across different cultural and linguistic contexts. He would later refer to Germany as his “scientific mother country,” affirming the significance of this period in shaping his intellectual development.

A lesser-known aspect of Rizal's stay in Germany is that, alongside his many forays into the then-new and exciting field of anthropology, he also donated 21 pieces of Philippine material culture to the Berlin Museum of Ethnology through Fedor Jagor. This fact came to light only in 2009, when Belgian Rizal scholar Lucien Spittael discovered a letter from Rizal to museum director Adolf Bastian, dated July 1888, confirming the donation. Spittael later verified that all 21 objects remained in the museum's collections.

This small cache of objects offers a fresh perspective on Rizal's intellectual pursuits. It shows that his engagement with anthropology extended beyond reading, attending lectures, and correspondence: he also contributed material culture to the field's early conversations.
