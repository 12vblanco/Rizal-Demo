The finger-bladed knife is a farming tool used in rice harvesting in the Philippines and other parts of Southeast Asia. Traditionally, it is used to cut panicles one by one. This knife is composed of a crescent-shaped metal blade attached crosswise to a wooden handle made of bamboo or hardwood. It is used by holding it in the right hand while holding the rice stalk in the left. The knife is called rakem in Ilokano, rakam in Isneg, lakom in Kalinga, lakem in Bontoc and Lepanto Kanakanay, yatab in Tagalog, gata in Bikol, and kayog in Hiligaynon and Bisaya.

The knife donated by Rizal, which he called pang-ani, is made of a plain, cylindrical-shaped wooden handle and a handlebar-shaped metal blade with serrated edges that resemble the wings of a bird. The animal carvings found in finger-bladed knives are attributed to the religious aspect of farming. Prior to the arrival of the Spaniards, rice was not consumed by the population all year round. Compared to other staples like root crops, rice cultivation was meticulous, with each step steeped in religious significance. Among the Ifugao of northern Luzon, it is believed that spirits live in rice grains, and that the use of finger-bladed knives causes minimal disturbance to the spirits.

Unlike a sickle, which cuts more rice stalks at one time, finger-bladed knives cut panicles one by one, making it possible to harvest ripened and unripe panicles separately. In traditional farming communities in Southeast Asia, the use of finger-bladed knives allowed more farmers to participate in the harvesting season, providing a source of income for the landless. Some research suggests that the use of finger-bladed knives also plays a role in selecting seeds with desirable qualities.

## Finger-Bladed Knives in the Time of Rizal

Spanish colonization transformed how agriculture was practiced in the Philippines. As a form of colonial control, the Spaniards reorganized early settlements into pueblos and divided vast lands into haciendas, where early Filipinos worked as farmers. Meanwhile, Spanish friars introduced new farming techniques and tools to increase yield. Sharecroppers in vast haciendas started using carabaos and plow technology to cultivate more rice, and sickles as their harvesting tool.

By the time Rizal made his donation in 1887, more efficient harvesting methods had already replaced finger-bladed knives, particularly in areas around Manila. However, in other rice-producing areas like Pangasinan and Ilocos, where farmers cultivated awned rice varieties, they continued to use finger-bladed knives. Farmers also used, and still use, finger-bladed knives in the Cordillera Region.

## Rizal and Agriculture

As a child of inquilinos (leaseholders) in the Dominican-owned Hacienda de Calamba in Laguna, Rizal was exposed to agriculture at a young age. He developed an inclination toward natural science and agriculture as much as his interest in arts and letters. His novel El Filibusterismo, which reveals the injustices in the 19th-century Philippines, included the plight of farmers through the character of the farmer-turned-bandit Cabesang Tales. In 1888, Rizal wrote the poem "Himno al Trabajo," which emphasizes agriculture's role in the country's progress.

Perhaps no work better showcases Rizal's agricultural pursuits than his 1881 achievement, when he obtained his perito agrimensor y tasador (land surveyor) degree. While exiled in Dapitan, he actively used his degree by creating the relief map of Mindanao. In 1893, Rizal purchased a piece of land in Barrio Talisay, where he built his house, a clinic, and a school. There, Rizal planted coffee, pineapple, and corn, in addition to maintaining fruit trees. He also helped develop Dapitan's first water system.
