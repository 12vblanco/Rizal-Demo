The sulpakan, a Tagalog portable fire piston, represents an ingenious fire-making technology rooted in Southeast Asia and the Pacific Islands. Contrary to the common online belief that José Rizal invented the object, historical and ethnographic evidence shows that the fire piston is an ancient tool widely used throughout Southeast Asia, long before Rizal's time. Fire pistons were traditionally made of bamboo, horn, ivory, bone, metal, and hardwood, and were used by various ethnolinguistic groups across the Philippines.

Among the 21 objects Rizal sent to the Ethnological Museum of Berlin in 1888, the sulpakan was the only item accompanied by a detailed description. Rizal described it in German as being made of buffalo horn and included a schematic diagram demonstrating how the tool functioned.

Its remarkable precision reveals expert craftsmanship in shaping, fitting, and decorating delicate materials. The pinpoint holes on both ends suggest that it underwent a turning process akin to that seen in 19th-century wooden balusters still visible in Filipino architecture today.

Based on Rizal's letter, the sulpakan may have originally been accompanied by yesca (tinder) powder, which was found missing. The device held particular relevance among swidden agricultural communities, where quick and reliable fire-making tools were essential for daily life.

One year earlier, on September 26, 1887, Rizal had also sent a sulpakan to his friend Ferdinand Blumentritt. In that same letter, he informed Blumentritt of the death of his sister, Olimpia, due to childbirth. The sulpakan was part of a small package that also included cigarettes, sampaguita, cinnamon, and kamuning flowers.

He explained: "The tinder is placed at the extreme of the stick 'a'. This tinder is held in 'd'. Afterwards, the stick is inserted in the hole 'c', it is pushed vigorously and withdrawn immediately, and now you have the fire. In order that it may slip in more smoothly, the stick is greased ('e')."

Rizal also explained the device's mechanism as based on the principle of compressed air, and noted the etymology of the term from the Tagalog root word sulpak, meaning "to insert a stick through a hole."

The UP Diksiyonaryong Filipino defines sulpak as the act of inserting a hard object into a hole, and as a device for producing fire using a tube made from carabao horn, hardwood, ivory, and similar materials. Tracing further from the Vocabulario de la lengua tagala by Juan de Noceda and Pedro de Sanlucar, published in 1613, the term may be connected to solpa, referring to the act of inserting one piece of short bamboo into another. The root syllable sul- or sol- may derive from a Proto-Austronesian origin, possibly linked to the reconstructed root *surup ("to enter, penetrate"), suggesting a linguistic continuity across many Philippine languages. For instance, Tagalog sulop ("to penetrate") aligns with Cebuano sulod ("to go in") and Kapampangan sulud ("to put on"), all of which reflect a core semantic field of inward movement or insertion.

Thus, in the word sulpak (or solpak), as in the fire piston, we find the convergence of two semantic domains: insertion (sul-) and audible force or impact (-pak). The term encapsulates its function: to forcefully push air and material into a chamber (sul), creating a burst of sound or combustion (pak). This is also why sulpak or sumpak is the common name for makeshift toy guns in many Filipino communities, referring to a different object that similarly involves force and sound.

In his response dated November 15, 1887, Blumentritt expressed sorrow over Olimpia's passing and noted his fascination with the sulpakan. He planned to present it to physics professors as an argumentum vivum, a living proof of the "great talent of the indios," remarking that the gift not only enriched his Philippine collection but also expanded his vocabulary with new, unknown terms.

Rizal's donation of these objects to a foreign museum reflects his deeper commitment to affirming Filipino cultural and historical identity. By presenting such artifacts abroad, Rizal sought to challenge colonial portrayals of indios as uncivilized or lacking culture. His scholarly knowledge of ethnology and the Malay archipelago offered proof that Filipinos were part of a dynamic, civilized world with inherent capacities for creativity and technological sophistication. The sulpakan thus becomes not just a utilitarian tool, but a powerful counter-argument against colonial discourse.

## A Southeast Asian Invention

The fire piston, also called a fire syringe, as stated by Rizal, operates on the principle of air compression. A piston is rapidly driven into a cylinder, compressing the air inside until it ignites a small amount of tinder, such as lulug or other flammable fibers. Its function is based on the same thermodynamic principle used in modern diesel engines, an invention whose creator, Rudolf Diesel, reportedly drew inspiration from this Southeast Asian mechanism.

Though the precise origin of the fire piston remains unclear, its presence in the Philippines predates European patents and usage. In the National Ethnographic Collection, fire pistons or syringes appear in several forms, such as the hulpakan or pinki among the Ifugao (made from carabao horn and wood) and the sulpak of the Tagalog from Sta. Maria, Laguna (made of wood). A toy gun-shaped sulpak from San Miguel, Bulacan (made from carabao horn) was used as a cigarette lighter during the Japanese occupation. These Tagalog variants employed lulug, a palm-based tinder with suha (pomelo) ashes, and used sebo (carabao tallow) as lubricant, echoing Rizal's original description of grease facilitating the stick's movement.

From the Cordillera region, the Tinguian people of Luba, Abra, also used fire pistons called sitoc or sugpec, which incorporated sawing motions to produce friction and heat. These were also used by Mountain Christians, Ilocanos, and Aetas of Villaviciosa, Abra, based on museum records.

British archaeologist Henry Balfour's 1908 report includes illustrations of fire pistons from various groups in North and South Luzon. He also noted that such objects could be found in the Dresden Museum, Vienna Museum, Berlin Museum, and the Ethnological Museum in Rome.

## Colonial Prejudice

The fire piston's global narrative reveals how Western and European scholars often dismissed the intellectual capacities of Southeast Asian communities despite encountering this sophisticated indigenous invention.

In his 1898 book The Philippine Islands and Their People, American colonial official Dean C. Worcester describes fire pistons crafted from buffalo horn in northern Luzon. Reflecting the colonial mindset of his time, he remarks: "To perform this operation successfully requires long practice. I have yet to see a white man who professes to be able to do it… how the savages first came to think of getting fire in such a way is, to me, a mystery."

Even as fire pistons were observed in practical use across the region, foreign scientists struggled to accept the idea that "primitive" peoples could independently develop such a device.

Henry Balfour exemplified this skepticism, noting that it seemed "almost incredible" that Eastern peoples could have discovered such a method by accident or gradual refinement. Yet pre-1907 records show that the fire piston was widely used throughout Burma, French Indo-China, the Malay Peninsula, Sumatra, Borneo, and the Philippine Islands, suggesting a long and deeply rooted history of use that could not have stemmed from European transmission.

Interestingly, some theorists proposed that the fire piston may have originated from the use of mortars and pestles for betel nut, though this is unlikely due to the different mechanical requirements of each tool. A more plausible theory points to accidental discovery during blowgun-making, where the act of boring might have triggered ignition due to air compression, an insight potentially shared by cultures across Southeast Asia.

By the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Western discourse often remained centered on European innovation despite the prevalence of fire pistons in Asia. Yet figures like Rizal challenged this narrative through concrete cultural evidence. For Rizal, donating a sulpakan was a political act: to assert that Filipinos, far from being passive colonial subjects, were creators, thinkers, and inventors. The sulpakan ignited pride, a living artifact, an argumentum vivum, of people's brilliance.
