The Philippines holds a highly diverse tangible and intangible cultural heritage, passed down through generations. This heritage includes traditional ornaments, common in different ethnolinguistic groups, which people often make from natural plant materials, animal bones and teeth, animal hair, shells, glass, and stones. People make some of these ornaments from beads.

Beads are small perforated objects of various shapes and colors, often made from glass, stone, plant seeds, and sometimes animal bones or teeth (or even plastic in recent times). These objects are usually strung together to form ornaments or jewelry. In some cultures, beads are associated with religious beliefs or spiritual practices used for prayers. They can also function as an amulet or talisman, signifying a connection to nature and spirituality in traditional belief systems.

Beads also played a significant role in intercultural and economic exchanges during the period of ancient maritime trade, which made people know and enculturate every type of bead in different parts of the world.

Currently, available information links how beads reached the Philippines to ancient trade routes, as no physical evidence proves the existence of the necessary technology to make them in the country during that time. Composition analyses conducted in different studies on beads proved that the materials possibly came from mainland Southeast Asia, East Asia, Western Asia, the Indian subcontinent, and Europe.

Stone and glass make the most durable beads. Affluent community members distinctly find and own trade beads with various colors and patterns, often referred to as heirloom beads, among the ethnolinguistic groups of Northern Luzon and Mindanao, making them symbols of wealth and status since pre-Spanish colonization. From ancient to present times, beads represent a shared heritage and historic past.

Heirloom beads of Northern Luzon are highly concentrated in the Cordillera Administrative Region; community leaders, often members of affluent families, succeed them as keepers and educators of heritage and traditions. The region names every bead it holds. The lingling-o (Ifugao, lingling-o; Kalinga, lubai or lufay; Bontok, bitog or dinumog) is a pendant often made of precious metals, like gold, wood, or jade, that symbolizes fertility and is worn by both men and women. Trade beads, commonly referred to as bongor in Kalinga, often consist of agate, carnelian, and colorful glass beads. The adjongan (made of agate), carnelian, binukkawan or filam filay (blue and white glass beads), pang-o (glass bead with laminated gold), and the chumat or chevron glass beads are the most prominent beads in the region.

In the Southern Philippines, beadwork is often made from interlocked tiny glass beads, strung with multiple strands of threads in different interwoven levels, creating complex patterns incorporated into the overall designs. Most patterns or designs are symbols that people believe protect the wearer or tell a story about the community. The beadwork tradition is common among Mindanao's non-Islamic ethnolinguistic groups, mostly in the forms of belts and necklaces. These include the hilet lemimet or beaded belt (T'boli), slah or beaded necklace (B'laan), kulubata or beaded necklace (Mandaya), sinalapid or a datu's belt (Matigsalug-Manobo), and the Bagobo's lumbung beaded or metal chain belt (for women), bakus or fully beaded belt (worn by women), and akus or belt (worn by men). In some instances, artisans added metal ornaments, such as the tiger bells of Mindanao, made from either brass or silver.

## Rizal's Beadwork Collection

The beaded ornament from Rizal's collection resembles the beadwork of the Southern Philippines in bead size and style; it is similar to the region's beaded belts, sashes, and body beads.

The Matigsalug-Manobo refer to their beaded belt as sinalapid, a traditional belt with colors and diamond patterns resembling a python's skin, worn exclusively by datus or chieftains. Though sinalapid was traditionally made from animal skin or hide, usually a python's, the beaded form constitutes a modern design. The Bagobo have the bakus, a fully beaded belt for women with a backing system where artisans sewed or attached each bead; the akus, on the other hand, is an elaborate belt or sash for Bagobo men.
