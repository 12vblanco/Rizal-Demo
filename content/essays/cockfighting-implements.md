In Noli Me Tángere, Rizal portrayed cockfighting as a social gathering. Sunday afternoons were incomplete without cockfighting during that time. People, regardless of social status, gathered and participated. Cockfighting drew people from all social classes, from the wealthy to the poor, and even the guardia civil and clergymen, all hoping to win. It represented both aspirations and pleasures: the desire to get rich easily and the amusement they derived from every blow of battling roosters. Women rarely participated in cockfighting, making it an almost exclusively male gathering; mostly, women sold cigars, foodstuffs, and other goods, and served as collectors at the pintu, or entrance.

Cockfighting has existed since precolonial times and remains widespread in Southeast Asia. Chronicler Antonio Pigafetta observed it in Palawan in 1521. Historian Francisco Ignacio Alcina noted that in the Visayas, people included it in every festivity. In neighboring countries, it plays a significant role in the people's religious and spiritual life.

Rizal acknowledged that cockfighting was deeply rooted in Filipino culture, as the words sabong and tari in the Tagalog lexicon show. Sabong refers to cockfighting itself, while tari pertains to the gaff, the curved metal spur attached to the rooster's legs. According to Rizal, this provided enough proof of its pre-Hispanic existence. However, while Pigafetta observed betting during cockfights, Rizal claimed that the Spaniards "encouraged and perfected" it as a form of gambling. The Spanish institutionalized gambling during the colonization.

## Regulating Cockfighting

The Spanish colonial government acknowledged the problem and even took action to curb it. However, as Rizal mentioned in Noli Me Tángere, the government benefited financially, making it difficult to shut the practice down. The government claimed it used the revenue generated from this venture for projects like building bridges and schoolhouses, though, given the conditions of the time, such a claim is difficult to accept.

Rizal argued that the Spaniards corrupted and weaponized cockfighting, along with religion, to keep Filipinos distracted and ignorant. He added that this greatly contributed to the indolence of Filipinos that the Spaniards perceived and painted. In Chapter 46 of Noli, Rizal illustrates how the poor risked everything to get rich instantly. He narrates that people poured out resources and spent time raising their roosters, hoping to win. For them, this was the only way to escape poverty, especially during a time when opportunities were scarce for indios. People sought cockfighting and other forms of gambling as solutions to hardships rather than questioning the existing economic, political, and social structures that held them back. Because of this, Rizal claimed that the Spaniards caused Filipino indolence, as opposed to the Spanish claim that indolence was inherent.

Rizal also used cockfighting to show class distinctions during that period. The contrasting views of the poor and the rich toward cockfighting implied a wide gap in living conditions. The poor regarded it as their only hope of getting rich instantly, while the wealthy merely treated it as entertainment. These contrasting views clearly reflected the social hierarchy of the time.

Despite his blatant criticism, Rizal's inclusion of a tahid (gaff) or chicken tie cord for cockfighting in his donations to the Ethnological Museum of Berlin reflects his recognition of cockfighting's significance and place in Filipino culture. Rizal offered no explanation for including it. Resil Mojares (2013) suggested that Rizal's donation might stem from "his desire to contribute to scientific knowledge." This is plausible, since Rizal was exchanging thoughts and ideas with European academics and scientists and working on annotating Antonio Morga's Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas at the time. Furthermore, in the dedication of his annotation, he expressed his aim to "awaken the consciousness of the past… and to rectify what has been falsified and slandered" by explaining Morga's observations of the natives' behavior and lifeways.

Dr. José Rizal donated the cuerda para atar el gallo (roughly translated as "rope to tie a rooster") to the Ethnological Museum of Berlin. It comprises an abaca rope with a small piece of tortoiseshell, an intertwined cotton cord, and a carved triangular bone stake. People use it by clasping the intertwined cord to one of a chicken's legs to keep the bird in place. His eagerness to show that the Philippines' inhabitants possessed a form of civilization before the Spaniards arrived could have driven his donation. Perhaps, more than simply presenting a cultural practice, he may also have intended to show colonialism's impact on such traditions.
