Clothing serves as a form of identity and is generally utilitarian, depending on the wearer's day-to-day activities. In the 19th-century Philippines, clothing changed as Spaniards influenced it and as Filipinos responded to societal trends. Generally, due to humidity, people wear loose, comfortable clothes regardless of social status. Additionally, Filipinos wear the salakot, a common wide-brimmed Filipino hat, to protect themselves from rain or intense heat when they are in the fields or at sea. It varies in material, size, and shape, ranging from helmets to conical, pointed, or rounded mushroom shapes. The French visitor to the Philippines in the mid-1880s, Jean Mallat, described it as "shaped like an umbrella." For example, Ilocano traders who sell their textiles in their region typically wear loose shirts, mid-calf salaual (lower garment), anajao raincoats, and a small salakot with a string to secure and keep the headgear in place.

The Vocabulario de la lengua tagala indicates that the Spaniards came across salakot making, a pre-existing industry, when they arrived in the Philippines. The Spaniards defined salacot (Spanish spelling) as a hat made from straw, bamboo, and nipa leaves. The word also describes one's covering and wearing a head covering. Apart from early Filipinos' general use, the Philippines regularly exported hats, and this persisted in the 1860s and 1870s as an export product to California, alongside cordage, cigars, maize, garlic, rice, and chicken eggs.

Hat-making was a female-dominated enterprise among Kapampangans and Ilocanos; they made hats alongside mats, cigars, cigarette cases, fish nets, and baskets. Males, on the other hand, gathered the materials and sold the hats at a fixed price their wives determined. The price was based on the time the maker rendered, the kind of material, or the difficulty in procuring it.

From 1565 to 1898, the common folk did not limit salakot use; the hat became part of the elite clothing ensemble. Among the indios, people used salakot for practicality and resourcefulness, a contrast to the elite class, who wore it for fashion and prestige. Damian Domingo, Justiniano Asuncion, and Jose Honorato Lozano depicted this transcendence of the salakot as a prized possession in their 19th-century compilation of drawings, which featured the principalia class dressed in finery and donning their intricately customized hats.

As part of the Filipino identity, the principalia (composed of gobernadorcillos, cabeza de barangay, and ilustrados) adopted salakot made of prestigious materials like tortoiseshell or carabao horn and embellished them with gold or silver. The high value of the tortoiseshell and carabao horn used for the salakot's frame led people to export them to neighboring countries. People used tortoiseshells for jewelry items while they made items like armors and balisong (butterfly knife) from carabao horns. As for the embellishment, a finial called tampokoy surmounted the hat. Through chasing and repoussé, artisans decorated the hat with floral motifs and created a kalado effect. It is believed that artisans manufactured the finest salakot from Camarines and Albay in the Bicol Region, and Bulacan.

## A Stylish Staple

Ethnologist and Naturalist Fedor Jagor wore native garments and accessories during his stay in the Philippines, including a mushroom-shaped salakot with a detachable nito lamp resting on top, which he used during his excursions. His unique salakot captured the interest of indios and mestizos alike. Governor-General Eulogio Despujol also wore a jusi and a silver-mounted salakot instead of the usual livery, which gained popularity among the natives.

The salakot manifested the custom of gift-giving as a prized possession that people passed on through generations. Fernando Fulgosio's 1871 account states that the salakot became a status symbol, which indios highly valued, making them great presents. Such was the case when Rizal donated his own salakot to Bastian in 1888, the only item he noted as "Es gehörte mir" (it belonged to me). His salakot plata y asta was made from thinly sheeted carabao horn, joined by silver threads, and featured repoussé silver finials decorated with floral motifs. Inside is a woven headpiece made of rattan attached with an abaca cord.

By donating his own prized possession, Rizal elevated Filipinos' ingenuity in the technical know-how of making hats. This act presents Filipinos as a people with a distinct culture and tradition, aligning with the Humboldtian perspective of Bildung, which posits the process of self-formation and cultural development. In the salakot industry, Filipinos possessed the knowledge and understanding of their cultural heritage, which equipped them with a mastered specialized skill. Serving as both a piece for utilitarian and aesthetic purposes, the salakot showcases a tangible proof of Filipinos' innate talent and skill.
