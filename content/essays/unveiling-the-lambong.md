In his 1888 letter to Adolf Bastian regarding his donation of Philippine materials, Rizal listed a velo de mujer, which probably refers to this piece, commonly known as lambong or veil. This lambong that Rizal donated to his colleagues in Germany resembles what women wore in the 1800s, as paintings by Jose Honorato Lozano (ca. 1822–1825), Manila and its Environs: A Scene in Town, and Filipino 19th-century painter Justiniano Asuncion (1816–1901) depict. In these paintings, the artists depicted the veil as dark violet or blue, or blue-black, lustrous with a silky texture and appearance. Two small pompoms made of cotton thread protruded from the head when women wore the veil, and two elongated, rabbit-foot-like pieces of cloth draped from the crown. A light blue rectangular cloth, made from linen or cotton, was possibly obtained from trading with Arab or Chinese merchants. The lambong's color almost always complemented the dark color of the tapis (overskirt), suggesting one of its vital functions: as cover.

In his 1889 El Fok-Lore Filipino, Isabelo de los Reyes mentioned the lambong under four different circumstances in his observations of Ilocano daily life: as a tool for exorcism, as a cover among churchgoers, in a funeral where a widow's face was covered with a shawl or lambong, and as daily wear among women. The descriptions he used, velo negro y bruñido (burnished black veil) or velo de negro brillante (veil of glossy black), match this object Rizal donated.

A much earlier account of the lambong came from Juan Ravenet, a French artist who was part of the Malaspina expedition (1789–1794) in the 18th century. Ravenet made several illustrations of the Parian (Chinese district) in Manila, noting among others the devout beata, or woman churchgoer, covered by a lambong, a shoulder-to-ankle black or dark blue veil worn by the pious.

Veering away from Luzon, historian and Jesuit missionary Francisco Ignacio Alcina, who lived in the Visayas, wrote in 1668, noting a head cover women wore that fell to the shoulder, and an outer garment called lambog/lambon that covered the whole body like a veil. The lambog/lambon is possibly the veil that Alcina's illustration of a datu and his wife (binukot or kept maiden) shows.

## Why the Lambong Matters

What makes Rizal's donated lambong enigmatic is that the piece appears unfinished, since only the upper portion is closed and sewn along the edges, resulting in some loose fibers in the other portions. However, the holes and stains observed in the attached blue rectangular cloth suggest that someone actually used the lambong. From the 19th-century illustrations depicting its manner of use, the lighter blue piece of cloth directly covers the hair and outlines the wearer's face, making this part more susceptible to wear and tear.

Rizal did not specify the Philippine group or part where this lambong came from, but this piece is possibly part of the Tagalog ensemble that Rizal tried to assemble (baro at saya, tapis, and lambong) as 18th- and 19th-century paintings of women in the Philippines depict. Reyes also distinguished the longer Ilocano lambong, which covers the wearer from head to toe, from the Tagalogs' lambong, which only reaches a little below the waist. The Ethnologisches Museum in Berlin's conservators confirmed the lambong only reached the mannequin's waist when worn, further confirming that this might have been a lambong more associated with Tagalog women.

## Lambong in the Contemporary Philippines

Contemporary practices in some parts of Luzon, such as in Malolos, Bulacan, and Angono, Rizal, suggest the lambong's continuous yet evolving relevance in Filipino religiosity. The latag-lambong tradition during Domingo de Ramos, commonly known as Palm Sunday among Catholic faithful, has been part of the Holy Week observance to celebrate Jesus Christ's triumphant entry into Jerusalem. In Malolos, Bulacan, for example, during the latag-lambong (literally, to lay down the veil), devotees lay down pieces of textiles along the path to serve as a carpet for Jesus Christ's effigy during a procession that starts from a small chapel to the main church. The community aims to have as many participants as possible so that the connected fabrics reach the church.

Up until the late 1980s, Malolos devotees witnessed their grandparents participate in the latag-lambong, using their own lambong to make the long trail for the procession, a tradition they claim has existed as far back as they can remember. An undated old photo of women participating in the latag-lambong using the same, if not similar, lambong that Rizal donated, turned out to be taken at this very church in Malolos, Bulacan, the Sta. Isabel de Unggaria Parish, in the late 1980s, after parishioners recognized the assigned parish priest and some women in the photo. The Malolos parish priest also mentioned that people frequently used inuwak (likened to a crow) instead of the term lambong in the past to refer to this type of veil, possibly due to its color. His grandmother used to make her own baro at saya and lambong, and she also actively participated in the latag-lambong when she was still alive.

Nowadays, longer commercial fabrics have replaced the lambong due to its unavailability and the waning number of parishioners participating in the tradition. Some places, however, still fight to keep the tradition alive. In Angono, Rizal, members of the Samahang Latag Banig at Lambong-Angono continue to use personal lambong, along with mats, during Domingo de Ramos. Some devotees claim their relatives passed the lambong or banig on to them as part of their devotion. After using the lambong or banig, they keep it for use in the following year and the years after.

The lambong's survival through decades of fluctuating influence and changes from interactions with different communities attests to the value people embed in and accord it in Philippine society. Rizal might have seen this as an important cultural material worthy of showcasing due to its prominence during his time, whether in the secular aspect, as an inherent part of everyday wear, or in the spiritual realm, for churchgoers or in the observation of some rituals. This more than 100-year-old piece of cloth might be the only surviving example of its kind.
