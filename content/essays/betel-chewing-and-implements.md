Betel chewing is very common in the Philippines and its neighboring Southeast Asian countries, as the areca palm, the source of the betel nut, is naturally abundant in the region. It became a part of local culture in some areas of the country, as it plays a significant role in socialization to this day.

A set of betel chew or mamá usually consists of a quarter of a bunga or areca nut (Areca catechu), a bit of apog (lime) or tabon-tabon (Atuna racemosa), a piece of buyo or betel piper leaf (Piper betle), and a bit of tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum) or mascada. This produces red spittle when chewed. The Manobo and Higaunon communities in Mindanao chew betel nut and buyo together with powdered lime from burnt land snail shells (Pila spp.), tobacco, and rattan fruit. The bark of kalingang (cinnamon; Cinamon mindanaense) is sometimes added for flavor and aroma. Some prepare betel chew by mashing the ingredients together, using a small mortar and pestle, until it is reduced into a soft mass, a common practice among elderly Bagobo and Bukidnon men and persons with mutilated teeth.

## Betel Chewing Tradition

Also known as nganga in Tagalog, a betel chew is often used for personal consumption but is also shared as a gesture of friendship or hospitality with visiting guests, or used as part of a ceremonial offering. It was offered to visitors of wealthy families in earlier times, served on intricately designed trays and containers as a form of hospitality. Aside from eliciting camaraderie among people, it was also considered a necessity for facilitating favorable conditions in dealing with those in the spirit world. Among the Western Bukidnon Manobo, the mamá comprises part of the ceremonial offerings used to evoke the deities when seeking their assistance. Hence, betel nut offerings and the betel chewing tradition continue among different communities across the country.

The importance of betel chewing is evident through the containers specifically made for the ingredients of the mamá. People make small portable containers for betel chew ingredients: some made of wood with carvings, while more luxurious containers are made of brass with intricate designs and separate compartments for the lime and other ingredients. The presence of shell lime holders from ancient Palawan graveyards in Luzon may suggest that the practice of betel chewing was found among prehistoric inhabitants of the country. Among the Bagobo indigenous group in Mindanao, betel chews are packed along with other grave goods, collectively called onong, and buried with the deceased for the journey from the earth toward the land of the dead.

One of the 21 objects José Rizal sent to Dr. Adolf Bastian of the Ethnological Museum of Berlin in 1888 was a kalikut, a betel mortar made of a wooden tube with a poking rod, used for mincing and mixing betel leaves, areca nuts, and lime for a chewing concoction. This small mortar and pestle is commonly used to crush tobacco and betel nut by the elderly and those with severely damaged teeth. In a letter to German anthropologist Dr. Adolf Bernhard Meyer on March 5, 1890, Rizal mentioned buyo as one of the intoxicants consumed by Filipinos before the arrival of the Spaniards, in addition to local alcoholic beverages such as arak, nipa palm, and coconut wine. Also part of Rizal's collection in Berlin is a dried shell of bunga or areca nut used as a tooth cleaner.
