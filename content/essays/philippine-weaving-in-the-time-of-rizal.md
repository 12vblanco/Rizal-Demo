As Dr. Rizal acquainted himself with the intellectual circles in Germany in 1887 with Maximo Viola's help, the Philippines was also being introduced to the European continent's other side, albeit in a different manner.

The Exposición General de las Islas Filipinas in Madrid, Spain, in 1887, featured textiles from the Philippines crafted from various plant materials using various techniques and designs. The Exposition's catalog listed fabrics made from seda, piña, jusi, algodon, abaca, and sinamay, and those in the form of pañuelo, embroidered dresses, silk shawls, and blankets. Despite the Exposition's notoriety for making a spectacle out of Filipinos from the Cordilleras as part of a live exhibition (one that Rizal condemned in his letter to his dear friend Ferdinand Blumentritt), the showcase of locally made textiles underscored the diverse cultures and artistry in the cultural communities in the Pearl of the Orient. This intricate craftsmanship manifested through delicate fibers such as piña, abaca, and silk, as well as the intricacy of the weaving processes and styles.

Long before the Spaniards set sights on the islands in the 1500s, the practice was already existent, although concentrated in the households. Raw materials and fabrics made from abaca and cotton were used for daily clothing and later on for utility, such as abaca cordage and cotton sails, which were traded in the market. Rizal himself would also be involved in the cultivation and trade of abaca through his business partnership with Don Mariano Hamoy when he was in exile in Dapitan.

Weaving piña fabric, and its infusion with other plant fibers, would then be explored by the locals as the Spaniards brought cultivated piña to the islands, later resulting in the fine and elegant clothing and designs now equated as markers of Filipino identity. Western Visayas provinces particularly cultivated the Red Spanish (Bromelia pigna) variety. Weavers from Aklan, Antique, and Iloilo painstakingly extracted fibers from these plants to weave fabrics that created a demand in the local and global market. Weaving from homes then expanded into cooperatives, as the Spanish colonial government mandated the use of upright or foot looms in the region for faster production and increased profit in the early 1800s. What was once a daily and mundane household task was transformed into a large commercial enterprise facilitated through the Manila-Acapulco Galleon Trade.

Textile weaving in the Western Visayas reached its peak during Rizal's lifetime. From the 1840s until the 1870s, fabrics made from piña, jusi (raw silk), silk, or a combination of these together with abaca and cotton found their niche to cater to local and European tastes. Iloilo particularly was dubbed the Textile Capital of the Country. In 1854, Iloilo handwoven goods exported to Manila were valued at $400,000. A total of $20,000 worth of these textiles were exported to Europe through Manila annually. In the 1850s–1860s, about 50% of the exports from Iloilo were merely textiles amounting to almost one million dollars, including those exported to neighboring islands.

Apart from the beauty and craftsmanship involved in these textiles, the dictates of the trading and global economy of that time made these fabrics also appear in museum collections and expositions abroad in the late 19th century, when Rizal was in Europe.

## The Baro't Saya and Barong in Rizal's Collection

In a letter to Blumentritt on April 13, 1887, Rizal, who was then in Berlin, lamented having to go to museums in Germany to be able to learn about his motherland and the culture of his people while he was traveling around Europe with Maximo Viola. With their earlier correspondence regarding the Exposition in Madrid in 1887, Rizal found himself frustrated.

Part of the 21 objects that Rizal sent to the Museum für Völkerkunde were clothing associated with the Tagalogs, such as a traditional ensemble for men, the barong Tagalog (camisa de piña para hombre), and for women, the baro (camisa de jusi de mujer), saya (saya de jusi de mujer), tapis (tapis de seda de los Tagalog), and pañuelo (pañuelo de nipis de mujer).

The barong Tagalog and the baro't saya are usually made of piña fiber and sometimes combined with other fibers, such as silk, which adds to their refinement and elegance. The barong's pechera (front panel), as well as the pañuelo, are usually decorated with intricate embroidery done by hand. Common embroidery designs include calado (open work), flowers, leaves and vines, as well as those inspired by other local motifs. The baro is usually sheer, made of thin, gauzy, and sometimes dyed fibers, and thus needs the pañuelo for covering. The tapis, usually darker and shorter than the saya, is worn above the latter. Given the intricate process entailed in weaving fabrics made from delicate plant materials, only the well-off and elite could afford to buy and wear them for church and other celebrations.

While there are no photographs of Rizal wearing a barong, he was presumed to own one, referenced as barong gasa in his letters to his family on December 30, 1882. However, the barong donated in Germany was probably not used by Rizal, since his letter to Bastian did not mention ownership, unlike his note of Es gehörte mir (it's mine) for one of his donated objects. Rizal, being close to his mother and having nine sisters, most likely had numerous sources for the donated collection of women's clothing. His sisters Saturnina, Trinidad, and Josefa also managed a textile shop in Manila before the Japanese Occupation in 1941.

Did Rizal donate Filipino material culture to counter the Western narratives about Filipinos and their culture, provoked by what he had heard about the Exposition in Madrid and his visits to the German museums? On one hand, Filipinos were depicted as the uncivilized indio, a derogatory term used by the Spaniards to support their hegemonic dominance as a superior culture in their view. On the other hand, Filipinos were also exoticized, their peoples and cultures showcased in Europe through objects that should have been praised for their artistry and craftsmanship, and yet obscured by the fact that they came from the Far East. Many Filipinos may also have been ignored, not given space and showcased in museums, as expressed by Rizal in the lack of Filipino objects in the Museum of Artillery in Paris during his visit in 1882. Regardless of how the Filipinos were depicted, one can assume that Rizal's actions may have been done as a redress for his countrymen who were othered and silenced, his way of reclaiming identity as one with and of Los Indios Bravos.

## Mindanao Textile Weaving

Ethnolinguistic groups in the northern and eastern parts of Mindanao are known to have cultivated abaca (Musa textilis) or Manila hemp to weave their traditional clothing. Aside from abaca, they also use commercial cotton, decorated with elaborate and intricately designed patterns. The Bagobo, Blaan, Kulaman, Mandaya, and T'boli are well-known abaca weavers. The Bagobo are known for their pelangi, made by tying circles repeatedly and creating a pattern before dyeing; the Kulaman for tritik, which involves sewing patterns with wax thread before dyeing; and the Mandaya and T'boli for ikat (resist-dye), the latter in tri-color cloths.

Abaca is indigenous to the Philippines, mostly found in upland areas and interior parts of the country. It is used for making rope, strings, fish nets, baskets, bags, and mats, as well as traditional clothing. It is commonly extracted from a cut trunk by separating the sheaths using a sharp object, usually a knife, and pulling them off the trunk by hand. A makeshift extracting tool, known as dangunan among the Mandaya and kalod among the Blaan, is also used to make fiber extraction easier. Weavers remove the sheaths until they reach the inner layer, considered best for making textiles. Abaca fibers are generally called lanot by the Bisayan groups and indigenous peoples in Mindanao; the Manobo occasionally call them yanot, while the Blaan call them lutay. At present, most weavers purchase bundles of abaca fibers from other farmers to lessen the time spent preparing raw materials. After extraction, the lanot are sun-dried for two days, then combed to separate the fibers, pounded or rubbed to a smooth texture, dried for at least three days, and combed again. Women then segregate the thick and thin fibers and knot them together to make them longer and suitable for weaving, a step that also serves as bonding time between mothers and daughters, usually the first taught to children.

These abaca fibers are dyed using natural materials like plants, animals, minerals, fungi, and bacteria. In the Philippines, as in neighboring Southeast Asian countries, traditional dyes are derived from natural colorants, mostly from plants: yellow ginger or turmeric (Curcuma sp.) for yellow, morinda (Morinda sp.) for red, and indigo (Indigofera sp.) for blue-black dyes among the indigenous peoples of Mindanao.

The Blaan and T'boli refer to yellow ginger/turmeric as kunil, and the Mandaya as dul'law. Morinda is sikarig among the Bagobo, sikalig among the Mandaya, and loko among the T'boli. To produce a deep shade of yellow, kalawag (turmeric) is mixed with lime. Instead of indigo, indigenous peoples in Mindanao use knalum (Diospyros sp.) among the Blaan, k'nalum among the T'boli, and kanal'lum among the Mandaya.

The extraction and application of red from the morinda species M. citrifolia has been documented among the Bagobo, and earlier accounts, particularly in Mindanao, corroborate its long history and significance among these groups. For instance, its range of intensity in the tangkulo (headcloth) and clothing of Mindanao indigenous peoples' datus (chieftains) and bagani (warriors) indicates the number of lives they have taken for Talabusao, the war deity. The color red can also be derived from several plant sources, such as achuete (Bixa orellana), narra (Pterocarpus indicus), and sibukaw (Caesalpinia sappan), among others.

These textiles are important in the lives of the indigenous peoples of Mindanao, as markers of war, social rank, beliefs and practices, and cultural identity, among others.

Among the 21 objects donated by José Rizal to Berlin's Museum für Völkerkunde in 1888, seven textiles and one belt/girdle were from Mindanao. The majority of these are handwoven abaca cloths that highlight the mastery of Filipino weavers, as well as the intricate designs of their work, like the pelangi and tritik among the Bagobo, Kulaman, and Blaan.
